Concise root cause (what's happening)
- The GradFn stores a raw Tensor* pointer to its input. In Mean you create a temporary intermediate (sum) and pass it into DivideScalar; DivideScalar sets a GradFn that stores a pointer to that temporary. When Mean returns the temporary is destroyed and the pointer in the GradFn becomes dangling. At Backward time you therefore access a destroyed Tensor object (its members get overwritten / m_Arena becomes invalid) and Allocate asserts.

Why this only reproduces via Mean (and not when you tested DivScalarGradFn directly)
- When you tested DivScalarGradFn directly you passed a live Tensor that outlived the GradFn. In the Mean path the input to DivideScalar (the sum) is a temporary local that is destroyed at the end of Mean, so the pointer saved by the GradFn becomes invalid.

How to confirm quickly
- Log the address of the Tensor passed into DivideScalar and the address stored in the created GradFn.inputs vector at the time of SetGradFn; log again at Backward time. If addresses match but the object no longer contains the same internal fields (or is overwritten), you have a dangling-pointer.

Safe fixes (ranked)
1) Make GradFn own/hold shared ownership of inputs (preferred design):
   - Change inputs to std::vector<std::shared_ptr<Tensor<T>>> and pass shared_ptrs for any tensor you want a GradFn to reference. This guarantees the referenced Tensor stays alive until no shared_ptr remains.
2) Make GradFn store copies or the minimal data it needs:
   - Instead of pointing to the whole Tensor object, copy only what the GradFn needs (shape, indices, cached values). This avoids needing to keep intermediates alive.
3) Ensure you never create GradFns that reference temporaries:
   - Modify operations so you do not create a GradFn that points at a local temporary. Either allocate the intermediate on the heap and transfer ownership, or restructure Mean so the graph references the original input (A) rather than the transient sum.
4) Short-term diagnostic guard:
   - Add an IsInitialized() check on the allocator and/or debug logging in GradFn constructor to detect when pointers refer to temporaries or destroyed objects.

Concrete recommendation (next steps)
- Refactor GradFn to use std::shared_ptr<Tensor<T>> for inputs (and update SetGradFn call sites). If you cannot refactor everywhere now, pick the costly-but-safe route: have DivideScalar (and other ops that might reference temporaries) store a detached copy or heap-allocate the intermediate and transfer ownership into the GradFn so it cannot dangle.
